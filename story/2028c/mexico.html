Svalbard Seed Vault, Norway – Early 2028

Maria Hernandez’s rooftop garden was small, but it was thriving. Rows of tomato plants, leafy greens, and chili peppers stretched across the concrete rooftop of her apartment building. Plastic bottles cut in half served as makeshift planters, and old tin cans had been repurposed to grow herbs. It wasn’t much, but it was something. The community had laughed at Maria when she started the project a few months earlier, but now, as food prices skyrocketed and markets emptied, people began knocking on her door. “How did you do it?” asked Rosa, an elderly neighbor holding a basket in her hands. “I have an empty balcony. Can you teach me?” Maria smiled and handed Rosa a small bag of seeds. “Start with these. Tomatoes are hardy. We’ll figure out the rest together.” Word spread quickly, and within weeks, Maria’s building had transformed. Balconies, rooftops, and even window ledges were bursting with green. Children painted water cans with bright colors, and families gathered on the roof in the evenings to share tips and trade produce. Maria became the unlikely leader of what the neighborhood began calling “La Revolución Verde,” the Green Revolution. She started hosting workshops in the building’s courtyard, teaching people how to grow food in small spaces using whatever materials they had. One evening, Maria stood on the roof, looking out over the city. For the first time in weeks, she felt hope—not because their problems were solved, but because they had stopped waiting for someone else to fix them.
